Donor Glucose and Delayed Graft Dysfunction in Liver Transplantation

Does Donor Risk Index Modify the Association?

Published

Invalid Date

Introduction

Delayed graft dysfunction (DGD) is a complication that can occur following liver transplantation, and is generally associated with longer hospital stays, higher mortality, and worse long-term graft survival. Understanding what predicts DGD matters both for patient outcomes and for decisions about which donor organs to accept.

One commonly used tool for assessing donor organ quality is the Donor Risk Index (DRI), a composite score that takes into account factors like donor age, cause of death, and organ type to estimate the likelihood of graft failure. Higher DRI values indicate higher-risk donations. While DRI has been used to predict transplant outcomes, it is based on demographic and clinical information and does not include real-time physiological measurements from the donor.

Donor glucose is one variable that might provide additional information. High glucose levels in critically ill patients can be a sign of physiological stress, which could potentially affect organ viability before transplantation. That said, whether donor glucose is actually associated with DGD — and whether that relationship changes depending on how risky the donor already is by DRI — is not well established in the literature we reviewed.

This study looks at both of these questions using data from 572 liver transplant donors. The dataset includes donor glucose levels (recorded as a time-weighted average and log-transformed for analysis), the Donor Risk Index, donor sex, and whether delayed graft dysfunction occurred.

Research question: Does donor glucose level predict delayed graft dysfunction in liver transplantation, and does this association differ by donor risk index?

Methodology

Model Specification

Because delayed graft dysfunction is a binary outcome (1 = dysfunction, 0 = no dysfunction), we used logistic regression to model the probability of DGD. This is a standard approach for binary outcomes and lets us estimate how each predictor is associated with the odds of dysfunction while holding the others constant.

We fit two models. The first includes an interaction term between log-transformed glucose and DRI. We included this interaction because we wanted to test whether the relationship between glucose and DGD might depend on how risky the donor already is — for example, glucose might matter more for high-risk donors than low-risk ones:

\[\text{logit}(P(\text{DGD})) = \beta_0 + \beta_1\log_2(\text{glucose}) + \beta_2\text{DRI} + \beta_3\text{sex} + \beta_4\log_2(\text{glucose})\times\text{DRI}\]

where \(\text{logit}(P(\text{DGD}))\) is the log-odds of delayed graft dysfunction, \(\log_2(\text{glucose})\) is the log base-2 transformed time-weighted average donor glucose, \(\text{DRI}\) is the Donor Risk Index, \(\text{sex}\) is donor sex (female = 1), and the interaction term \(\log_2(\text{glucose}) \times \text{DRI}\) captures whether the glucose effect differs across DRI levels.

We also fit a reduced model without the interaction term, to look at the independent effect of each predictor:

\[\text{logit}(P(\text{DGD})) = \beta_0 + \beta_1\log_2(\text{glucose}) + \beta_2\text{DRI} + \beta_3\text{sex}\]

We then compared the two models using a likelihood ratio test (LRT) to see whether adding the interaction term meaningfully improved model fit. If the test came back non-significant (\(p > 0.05\)), we would conclude there is not enough evidence for an interaction and stick with the simpler model.

Choice of Predictors and Transformations

Glucose was log-transformed (base 2) before modeling because the raw values were right-skewed. The log\(_2\) scale is also convenient for interpretation: a one-unit increase corresponds to a doubling of the original glucose value.

We included donor sex as a covariate based on prior literature suggesting it may be related to graft outcomes. DRI was kept as a continuous variable rather than grouped into categories, since collapsing it would lose information.

Assumptions

Logistic regression requires a few key assumptions: that the log-odds of the outcome is approximately linear in each continuous predictor, that observations are independent, and that predictors are not perfectly collinear with each other.

We assessed linearity by looking at the shape of the fitted logistic curve, which appeared smooth and monotonic — consistent with the linearity assumption, though we did not run formal diagnostics. Independence seems reasonable since each row in the dataset is a separate donor-recipient pair. The three predictors capture different aspects of the donor (metabolic, composite risk, and demographic), so perfect collinearity is unlikely. It is worth noting that 135 observations were dropped due to missing data, leaving 437 complete cases, which is still a reasonable sample size for this kind of model.

Results

Model Comparison: Likelihood Ratio Test

The likelihood ratio test comparing the full model to the reduced model returned a \(p\)-value of 0.32. This means we do not have sufficient evidence at the 0.05 significance level to conclude that the interaction between glucose and DRI improves model fit. In other words, the data do not support the idea that the effect of glucose on DGD changes depending on the donor’s risk level. We proceeded with the reduced model for the rest of the analysis.

Looking at the reduced model, donor risk index was the only statistically significant predictor (estimate = 0.958, SE = 0.328, \(z\) = 2.92, \(p\) = 0.003). Donor glucose (\(p\) = 0.282) and donor sex (\(p\) = 0.878) were not significant at the 0.05 level.

Effect Size and Confidence Interval for Donor Risk

To better understand the size of the DRI effect, we exponentiated the coefficient to get an odds ratio of approximately 2.61 (95% CI: 1.40 to 5.07). This suggests that for each one-unit increase in DRI, the estimated odds of delayed graft dysfunction increase by about 161%, holding the other variables constant. The confidence interval is fairly wide, which reflects some uncertainty in the estimate, but it lies entirely above 1.0 — suggesting the positive association is reasonably consistent.

Discussion

Overall, our results suggest that donor risk index is a meaningful predictor of delayed graft dysfunction, while donor glucose does not appear to add much on top of what DRI already captures — at least in this dataset and with this modeling approach.

The odds ratio for DRI was about 2.61, which seems like a substantial effect, though we want to be careful about over-interpreting a single model with limited covariates. The confidence interval (1.40 to 5.07) is wide enough that there is real uncertainty around this estimate. Still, the direction is clear and the result is statistically significant, which gives us some confidence that DRI is picking up on something real.

The non-significance of glucose was somewhat surprising given our initial hypothesis, but there are a few reasonable explanations. It is possible that DRI already captures much of what glucose reflects — donors with high DRI scores tend to be older and sicker, which could also drive elevated glucose. If that is the case, glucose may not tell us much beyond what DRI already does. It is also possible that a glucose effect exists but that our sample of 437 complete cases was not large enough to detect it reliably, especially after 135 observations were dropped due to missingness. We cannot really distinguish between these two explanations with the data we have.

There are a few other limitations worth mentioning. Our model only includes three predictors, and there are likely other variables — such as cold ischemia time or recipient-level factors — that could matter and are not accounted for here. Because this is observational data, we also cannot make any causal claims. A higher DRI being associated with more dysfunction does not mean DRI causes it; both could be driven by underlying donor characteristics we are not measuring. We also handled missing data by dropping incomplete cases, which is simple but may introduce bias if the missingness is related to the outcome.

If we were to extend this analysis, multiple imputation could help recover some of the information lost from the 135 dropped observations. Including additional clinical variables would also likely improve the model. It would also be interesting to look at glucose at different time points rather than just a time-weighted average, to see if timing matters.

Conclusion

We used logistic regression to examine whether donor glucose predicts delayed graft dysfunction and whether that relationship is modified by donor risk index. The interaction between glucose and DRI was not statistically significant (LRT \(p\) = 0.32), and glucose itself was not a significant predictor in the reduced model (\(p\) = 0.28). Donor risk index, on the other hand, showed a consistent positive association with DGD across both models, with an estimated odds ratio of 2.61 (95% CI: 1.40 to 5.07).

Based on these results, DRI appears to be the stronger predictor of graft dysfunction in this dataset, and glucose did not provide meaningful additional information beyond it. That said, our model is relatively simple and there is quite a bit of uncertainty in some of our estimates, so these findings should be interpreted cautiously rather than as definitive conclusions. We are 95% confident that a one-unit increase in donor risk is associated with between 1.4× and 5.1× higher odds of graft dysfunction.
